FAQ

Are shortened links safe?

No. You should avoid ever clicking on a shortened link (especially from an unknown source) because you can't see where it actually goes. If you receive one, you should unshorten it to inspect it first.

That said, if using a link shortener is unavoidable, you can at least use one that isn't working against you. LinkZero is the safest option and it collects no data about you or the people who click your links, it has no ad network, no resale business, and no incentive to do anything other than forward one URL to another.

Can I shorten any URL?

Almost. URLs that use dangerous patterns or known malware, phishing, and scam domains are blocked. Legitimate URLs should pass without issue.

Can I unshorten a link?

Yes. Paste any shortened link and click Unshorten to reveal the final destination without your browser ever visiting it. The lookup is performed by our server, so your IP address is never exposed to the destination or any trackers embedded in the redirect chain.

Do links expire?

No. Links are permanent. Since nothing is tracked, there's no activity data to expire them on, and no reason to delete them.

Can I choose a custom short code?

No. Codes are randomly generated. Custom aliases create moderation burden, and random codes are more resistant to enumeration.

If I shorten the same URL twice, do I get two links?

No. Submitting a URL that has already been shortened will return the existing short link. There's no benefit to creating duplicates.

What data do you collect?

None. Only the mapping between a short URL and its destination are stored. That's it. No IP addresses, no click counts, no timestamps, no referrers, no cookies, no analytics. When you shorten a link, we don't know who you are. When someone clicks it, we don't know who they are either.

Can I report a malicious link?

Yes. Email us with the short URL and why you believe it's malicious. If warranted, it'll be removed.

Why should I trust you?

You shouldn't. You shouldn't trust anything or anyone blindly. The code is public on GitHub, so you can verify exactly what it does and doesn't do, but that still means you have to trust the server. You can always self-host if you'd rather remove trust from the equation altogether.

With that out of the way, I can tell you the reason I created this tool is the same reason I often build my own solution, because nothing elegant nor privacy-friendly enough existed yet. I build tools I need to use myself.
